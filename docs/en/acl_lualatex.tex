\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This report presents a Vietnamese news recommendation system based on graph representation learning, focusing on two common issues in news data: \textbf{sparsity} and \textbf{cold-start}. Using comment/reply interactions from VnExpress, we build a data crawling pipeline and conduct EDA, design three graph structures \textbf{G1--G3} (expanded toward Social and Semantic signals), benchmark modern graph-based recommenders (LightGCN, SimGCL, XSimGCL, LightGCL), and propose \textbf{HGN} (a heterogeneous multi-aspect GNN) along with an exploratory \textbf{HCL} (Heterogeneous Contrastive Learning) setting. Evaluation is performed under two protocols: \textbf{Full Ranking Cold-Start} and \textbf{Leave-One-Out (Loo100)}, using Recall@10, NDCG@10, Hit@10, and MRR. Results indicate that expanding the social graph yields clear benefits for heterogeneous models, while content-based signals remain crucial under cold-start.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Digital news platforms continuously generate a large volume of content over time, making it difficult for users to find relevant articles quickly. Recommender systems play a key role in personalizing content, increasing engagement, and improving user experience. However, news has several unique characteristics: a short life cycle, rapidly changing trends, and highly sparse interactions because most readers do not leave explicit traces.

In this project, we approach Vietnamese news recommendation using two primary signal sources:
(i) \textbf{user--article interactions} (comments) that directly reflect preferences,
(ii) \textbf{user--user reply relations} that reflect social influence or opinion similarity in discussions.
Accordingly, we build a graph-based and heterogeneous-graph-based system to separate relationship semantics and fuse them in a controlled manner.

\textbf{Main contributions}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We build a VnExpress data collection pipeline including articles, metadata, comments/replies, and user profiles, and perform EDA to characterize sparsity and power-law behavior in interactions.
  \item We design three graph layers \textbf{G1--G3} to study the effect of topology expansion: G1 (bipartite), G2 (adding social edges), and G3 (adding category hubs).
  \item We benchmark modern graph recommender models including LightGCN~\citep{he2020lightgcn}, SimGCL~\citep{yu2022graph}, XSimGCL~\citep{yu2023xsimgcl}, and LightGCL~\citep{cai2023lightgcl}, and compare them with content-based baselines.
  \item We propose \textbf{HGN} (dual-path propagation) and explore \textbf{HCL} (heterogeneous + contrastive) to study robustness under sparse data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{GNNs for recommendation.}
LightGCN~\citep{he2020lightgcn} is a popular baseline due to its simplified GCN architecture for recommendation: it removes nonlinearities and trainable transformation matrices, keeping only linear message passing and multi-layer embedding aggregation.

\paragraph{Graph contrastive learning.}
Under sparse data, contrastive learning improves embedding robustness by constructing different ``views'' and optimizing InfoNCE. SimGCL~\citep{yu2022graph} perturbs embeddings during propagation; XSimGCL~\citep{yu2023xsimgcl} aims for an extremely simple and efficient design. LightGCL~\citep{cai2023lightgcl} introduces a global-context view to compensate for limitations of purely local neighborhood learning.

\paragraph{Heterogeneous recommendation.}
When the graph contains multiple node/edge types, homogeneous models may mix relationship semantics. HGRec~\citep{shi2020hgrec} proposes meta-path-based aggregation and multi-semantic fusion, which is suitable for data containing both preference and social signals.

\subsection{Summary statistics and sparsity}
The collected dataset contains \textbf{3,645} articles, \textbf{15,693} users, and \textbf{40,045} interactions (comments/replies). On average, there are about \textbf{2.55} interactions per user and \textbf{10.99} interactions per article.

Sparsity of the user--article interaction matrix:
\begin{equation}
\text{Sparsity} = 1 - \frac{40{,}045}{15{,}693 \times 3{,}645} \approx 99.93\%.
\label{eq:sparsity}
\end{equation}
This level of sparsity indicates a high cold-start risk: most users have small degrees, making purely collaborative models challenging.

\section{Graph Design and Expansion Strategy (G1--G3)}
\label{sec:graph}

\subsection{Overall idea}
With extremely high sparsity (\eqref{eq:sparsity}), a pure user--article graph struggles to propagate information for low-activity users. Therefore, we design three graphs with increasing signal richness:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{G1 (Bipartite):} users and articles with interaction edges.
  \item \textbf{G2 (Social expansion):} adds user--user edges from reply relations in discussions.
  \item \textbf{G3 (Semantic hubs):} adds category nodes to shorten paths between users and groups of articles within the same category.
\end{itemize}

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem definition and notation}
Let $U$ be the set of users and $A$ be the set of articles. The goal is to predict the relevance between a user $u$ and an article $a$ based on interaction history, then rank and recommend Top-$K$ articles that the user has not interacted with.

\subsection{Graph baselines}
\paragraph{LightGCN.}
LightGCN propagates embeddings linearly via a normalized adjacency:
\[
\mathbf{E}^{(k)} = \hat{\mathbf{A}} \mathbf{E}^{(k-1)}, \quad
\mathbf{E}^{*}=\sum_{k=0}^{K}\alpha_k \mathbf{E}^{(k)}.
\]
This is an effective baseline for recommendation on interaction graphs.

\paragraph{SimGCL / XSimGCL / LightGCL.}
Contrastive models add an InfoNCE objective to yield more robust embeddings under sparsity. SimGCL introduces perturbations during propagation. XSimGCL simplifies the design to reduce cost while preserving contrastive benefits. LightGCL injects global context to overcome limitations of purely local neighborhoods.

\subsection{HGN: Multi-aspect heterogeneous GNN}
The data contains at least two different relations: user--article interactions (preference) and user--user replies (social). HGN splits propagation into two branches:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Interest path}: propagation on $\mathcal{G}_{u-a}$.
  \item \textbf{Social path}: propagation on $\mathcal{G}_{u-u}$.
\end{itemize}
Then it fuses them by summation:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_u = \mathbf{h}_{int} + \mathbf{h}_{soc}.
\label{eq:fusion}
\end{equation}
This design avoids mixing relation semantics while keeping the pipeline simple for training on sparse data.

\subsection{HCL: exploratory heterogeneous contrastive learning}
HCL combines heterogeneity (branch separation) with contrastive loss. Under sparse regimes, contrastive learning can be sensitive to negative sampling and temperature, causing training instability. We keep HCL as an exploratory variant to observe and compare stability.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Training configuration}
Models are trained for 100 epochs with batch size 2048. The main loss is BPR for implicit feedback; contrastive models additionally include InfoNCE.

\subsection{Evaluation protocols}
\paragraph{Full Ranking (cold-start).}
Users are split 80/20. On the test set, the system ranks candidate articles and reports Recall@10, NDCG@10, Hit@10, and MRR.

\paragraph{Leave-One-Out (Loo100).}
For each test user, we select 1 positive interaction and 99 randomly sampled negatives to evaluate ranking quality within a small candidate set.

\subsection{Metrics}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Recall@10}: fraction of relevant items retrieved in top-10.
  \item \textbf{Hit@10}: whether any relevant item appears in top-10.
  \item \textbf{NDCG@10}: rewards relevant items ranked higher.
  \item \textbf{MRR}: sensitive to the rank position of the first relevant item.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why is social expansion (G2) helpful?}
Social edges increase connectivity among users even if they do not interact with the same articles, enabling better embedding propagation under sparse data. With the dual-path design, social signals are exploited without corrupting direct preference signals.

\subsection{Why can category hubs (G3) be unstable?}
Categories are coarse-grained. When users connect to categories and categories connect to many articles, the model may over-recommend ``same-category'' items with weak personalization, leading to over-generalization in some settings.

\subsection{Why is HCL unstable?}
In sparse regimes, few positives and sensitive negative sampling make InfoNCE hard to stabilize. When combined with heterogeneity (two propagation branches), balancing losses and hyperparameters becomes harder, often causing convergence instability.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Conclusion}
We build a Vietnamese news recommender based on graph learning from VnExpress data: crawling pipeline, EDA, three graph variants G1--G3, benchmarking modern graph models, and exploring heterogeneous modeling. Results show that social graph expansion significantly improves heterogeneous models, while content-based methods remain important under cold-start.

\subsection{Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Comment/reply-based data may be biased because many reads produce no interactions.
  \item Training cost increases with graph expansion, especially for heterogeneous models.
  \item The current model is static and does not handle online updates when new articles appear.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future directions}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Dynamic graph learning to capture time-varying preferences.
  \item Extend meta-paths with tags/authors/entities to provide finer semantic signals than categories.
  \item More stable self-supervised learning under extreme sparsity.
  \item Distillation/model compression to reduce inference cost in deployment.
\end{itemize}
